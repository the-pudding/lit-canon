What novels are studied from the 1990s?

Swipe Right

The first Harry Potter book was published 25 years ago. When it comes to whether a new generation will read the series, I wonder if it will go the way of Grapes of Wrath, The Catcher in the Rye, or The Great Gatsby: a classic that every teenager is required to read.

If Harry Potter ends up on English 101 syllabi, it's the epitome of its legacy; educators think it’s important enough to assign as required reading to Gen Z.

An Attempt at the turn-of-the-Century Literary Canon, Using Data from College Syllabi

By Matt Daniels

Using 2 million syllabi from the past 10 years, I looked at which books from the 1990s are, today, widely assigned in college-level English Literature classes.

Here’s the #1, most-assigned book, which appears among 1,200 syllabi.

The Things They Carried

Tim O’Brien

appears in ​​2,050 syllabi

I wasn’t familiar with The Things They Carried before researching it. But when I told people that it was the most-assigned book among ’90s fiction, many said, “that makes sense.”

Indeed it does make sense when you look at what this book has accomplished since it was published in 1990. It never was a New York Times bestseller, so it wasn’t commercially successful upon its release, but it did go on to be a Pulitzer Prize finalist.

NPR said it’s now a “a staple of college and high school English classes, celebrated as one of the most important books about the experience of war,” which helps explain why it’s the 24th most-rated ’90s book on Goodreads.

As we walk through the top ten books, I’ve added this context around how each book has performed outside of its frequency in English literature curriculum:

  • New York Bestseller: was a book commercially successful when it published?
  • Goodreads Ranking: it widely read today?
  • Literary Prizes: was it acclaimed by academics and critics?

Books like Harry Potter are cultural touchstones—we should expect book that are widely-read to one day by studied. Yet that’s rarely the case.

With the exception of Harry Potter and the Sorcerer’s Stone, few mega-popular books end up assigned in English Literature syllabi.

In 1951, Catcher in the Rye charted for 7 months as a New York Times Best Seller. Around the same time, Pulitzer Prize-winningThe Caine Mutiny, by Herman Wouk, would begin charting as well, and not leave the Best Seller List for 2.5 years.

There are countless examples of Caine Mutinys: Harry Potter-level popularity that eventually enter obscurity. Few songs from history are remembered by younger generations. Only a handful of movies will remain classics.

But with books, there’s the added forcing function of high school and college assigned readings, an opposing force to a natural fading of culture.
